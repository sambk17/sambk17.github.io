# Database Systems: The Hat Trick 🎩✨

They say the third time’s a charm, but in my case, it’s more like a masterclass in persistence. I've taken "Database Systems" three times across two major universities—**The University of Washington** and **Georgia Tech**.

Why? Because in the world of Data Science and ML Engineering, your models are only as good as the data you can fetch, and your pipelines are only as strong as the architecture supporting them. Whether I'm acting as a Data Scientist perfecting a SQL query or a Data Engineer orchestrating complex ETL workflows, the database is where the magic (and the headache) begins.

#### My Journey Through the Tables 📊

* **Round 1: The Foundation (UW, Winter 2014)** – I kicked things off with **[IS 445 (Database Management)](https://www.washington.edu/students/crscat/infosys.html)** under [Dr Manoj Parameswaran](https://foster.uw.edu/faculty-research/directory/manoj-parameswaran/). This was my entry point into real-world design, implementation, and transaction management.  
* **Round 2: Leveling Up (UW, 2015)** – During my MSIS program, I returned to Manoj’s classroom for **[MSIS 543 (Advanced Data Warehousing)](https://www.washington.edu/students/crscat/msis.html)**. We dove deep into Business Intelligence (BI) solutions, cloud-based apps, and performance tuning—skills I’d later use to architect cloud migrations for major clients.
* **Round 3: The Clincher (Georgia Tech, Fall 2023)** – Most recently, I tackled **[CS 6400 Database Systems Concepts and Design](https://omscs.gatech.edu/cs-6400-database-systems-concepts-and-design)**, taught by [Dr Leo Mark](https://sites.cc.gatech.edu/projects/XMLApe/people/leo.html) (now retired), as part of my **OMSCS program**. While the textbook only took us through Chapter 17 (Indexing Structures), the real test was the team project. After a "meh" Phase 1 (enhanced entity-relationship diagram), my team rallied for a 93% in Phase 2 (SQL queries) and absolutely crushed the final demo with a **98%** on our Flask, TypeScript, and PostgreSQL application.

#### A "Double Agent" in Grad School 🕵️‍♂️
Fun fact: While taking CS 6400, I was also enrolled in **[UW’s CS PMP](https://www.cs.washington.edu/academics/graduate/pmp/)** program. I learned the hard way that academic credits don't always play nice—UW's CS prof deemed my GT work "non-transferable" because of differing curriculum depths. While I'd argue that 1 semester vs. 1 quarter at least deserves *some* partial credit, I took it in stride. If anything, it just meant more time spent mastering the craft...not that I'm saying I'll take it a fourth time!

#### Pay It Forward: The CS 6400 "North Star" 🌟
The most valuable thing I can share isn't just my story—it's the code. I found existing repos for CS 6400 to be a bit "cloudy" when I was a student. To help future GT teams, I’m making my team's **Phase 1, 2, and 3** work accessible as a reference. 

Even though my access is now "frozen" since I've moved on from UW student status, the repository stands as a blueprint for success.

👉 **Check out the repo here:** [GT CS 6400 - Fall 2023 Reference](https://gitlab.cs.washington.edu/sambk/cs_6400_fall_2023/)

Whether you're a future student looking for a reference point or a fellow engineer who just loves a good PostgreSQL schema, I hope this helps you crush your next project just like we did. 🚀
